## Design Context

### Users
Two equally important audiences:
- **Ticket buyers** — regular people attending events, many with no crypto knowledge. They interact with the shop, ticket wallet, and QR code at the door. Need simplicity, reassurance, and zero jargon.
- **Event organisers** — create events, track sales, manage the dashboard. More comfortable with technology but not necessarily crypto-native. Expect clarity and efficiency.
- Doormen are a third, secondary role — they use the scanner briefly at the gate.

### Brand Personality
Friendly and accessible — the blockchain layer is invisible infrastructure, not a selling point to parade. Three words: **confident, clean, alive.**
Emotional goal: users should feel like they're holding something real and trustworthy, not a lottery ticket or a JPEG.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Dark theme** — non-negotiable, already established. Background `oklch(0.10 0.014 258)`, surface `oklch(0.14 0.014 258)`.
- **Green accent** `oklch(0.72 0.118 148)` — the single expressive colour. Used sparingly so it retains impact.
- **Typography**: Unbounded (display, weights 400/600/900) + Epilogue (body, 400/500). Do not change these.
- **Dotted background pattern** — 28×28px radial dot grid, part of the visual identity.
- **Aurora glow effect** — pulsing green blobs behind key UI surfaces (already on hero + ticket page).
- Anti-references: generic SaaS gray, crypto-bro neon chaos, Ticketmaster clutter.

### Design Principles
1. **Invisible complexity** — on-chain mechanics never surface in the UI. No wallet addresses in primary copy, no "mint" or "cNFT" jargon for buyers.
2. **Restraint with the accent** — green appears only on primary actions, status indicators, and brand moments. Overuse kills its power.
3. **Consistent spatial rhythm** — pages share the dotted bg, the same nav pattern, and a 28px/16px spacing cadence. Cohesion across buyer and organiser flows matters.
4. **Friendly confidence** — copy is short, direct, warm. Interfaces don't over-explain or hedge.
5. **Mobile-first interactions** — buyers use phones at concerts; doormen scan on phones. Desktop is secondary for buyers, primary for organisers creating events.
